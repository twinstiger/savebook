Welcome to Forza Horizon 6 Japan — a game that blends adrenaline-pumping street racing with deep car culture. If you're new to the series or just starting your Japanese racing journey, this guide covers everything you need to go from complete beginner to confident driver. We'll walk through controller setup, core driving mechanics, the economy system, and the best starter cars so you can hit the road running.

Unlike traditional racing games, FH6 gives you massive freedom. You can ignore the main story entirely and spend hours hunting barn finds, perfecting drift technique on touge roads, or competing in seasonal championships. But having a solid foundation makes everything more enjoyable — and profitable.

Controller Setup: Keyboard vs. Gamepad

Your control scheme fundamentally shapes your driving experience. FH6 supports both keyboard and controller input, with each offering distinct advantages depending on your racing style.

Gamepad (Xbox/PlayStation Controller)

A controller is strongly recommended for competitive racing. The analog triggers give you precise throttle and brake control — essential for techniques like trail braking into hairpins or feinting into corners. Set your trigger sensitivity to 50% dead zone to eliminate drift at the start of inputs, and enable vibration feedback for tire grip monitoring.

Recommended button mapping: A/X to accelerate, B/Circle to brake, X/Square for handbrake (critical for initiating drift on RWD cars), and LB/L1 for clutch if you're running a manual transmission tune. The bumpers work well for shifting gears up and down in manual mode.

Keyboard

Playing on keyboard is challenging but doable, especially for casual cruising. W for throttle and S for brake are straightforward, but the lack of analog input means you can't modulate throttle smoothly — critical for maintaining drift angles on touge sections. If you must use keyboard, consider binding handbrake to Space for drift initiation. Keep in mind that keyboard players face a real disadvantage in online competitiveness, but the game is fully playable solo.

Steering Assist Settings

New players should start with moderate steering assist. This isn't "easy mode" — it simply limits your maximum steering angle at high speed to reduce spin-outs. As you build intuition, lower the assist gradually. Advanced players often run with zero steering assist and learn to countersteer manually. The braking assist is worth keeping on at least low for the first 10 hours while you learn track layouts.

Driving Fundamentals: Steering, Throttle & Braking

Forza Horizon 6's physics engine rewards smooth, calculated inputs over aggressive corrections. Japan's technical tracks — especially the mountain touge passes — punish ham-fisted driving immediately.

Steering Logic

The golden rule: turn the wheel before you need to, not when you need to. In racing, you're always steering toward where you want to go, not where you are. This sounds obvious, but beginners often stare at the car hood and turn when they see the corner approaching. Look further ahead, and your steering naturally becomes timely.

On FH6's mountain touge routes, this is especially critical. Hairpin corners arrive fast, and overthinking leads to brake-checking mid-corner — a guaranteed spin on low-grip surfaces. Commit to your line early, and use the full width of the road to carry speed.

Throttle Management

Flat throttle exiting a corner is the fast way on gentle curves, but dangerous on touge hairpins. The key is progressive throttle application: apply 30-40% as you straighten the wheel, then roll on more power as the car aligns with your exit path. On AWD cars this is more forgiving; on RWD cars, too much throttle too early will spin you out as the rear steps loose.

For turbocharged cars, watch your boost gauge. Turbo lag means maximum power arrives 0.3-0.5 seconds after you floor the throttle. On short straights between corners, plan to pre-spool the turbo by getting on throttle slightly early so full boost is available the moment you need it.

Braking Zones

Study the track and pre-determine your braking points — don't wing it. For a 100km/h corner entry, start braking 50 meters before the turn-in point. Trail braking (continuing to brake lightly while turning in) rotates the car and lets you carry more speed to the apex. It's an advanced technique that takes practice but separates good drivers from great ones on technical circuits.

The handbrake is your drift initiation tool, not your stopping tool. Beginners misuse it, thinking it's for slowing down. It's not — it's for breaking rear traction intentionally so you can redirect the car's nose while sliding. Use it only when you want to initiate a drift, not for regular corner entries.

Economy System: Credits & Forzathon Points

FH6 has a dual-currency system: Credits (CR) for buying cars and upgrades, and Forzathon Points (FP) for purchasing exclusive items in the Forzathon Shop. Understanding how to earn both efficiently is crucial for long-term progression.

Credits

Credits come from race wins, championships, PR stunts, and board smashing. Early game, complete every race and championship you encounter — the CR rewards compound fast. The Showcase events (celebrity races against trains, jets, etc.) award substantial CR for their difficulty and are worth clearing early.

Avoid spending CR on expensive cars too early. Invest in a solid mid-range car (2-5 million CR) and upgrade it properly. A well-tuned car in A-Class will outperform a stock S1-class car that you can't afford to maintain. Upgrade your chosen car in stages: handling first (suspension, brakes, weight reduction), then engine/powertrain.

Forzathon Points

FP accrues passively as you drive and complete challenges, but the rate is slow — typically 1-2 FP per race. Bonus FP events double or triple your earnings for limited windows. The weekly Forzathon Shop refreshes with exclusive cars (often 500-800 FP) and clothing items.

Complete all Festival Playlist activities each season — they grant bonus FP on top of regular earnings. The "Forzathon Daily Challenge" gives 5 FP just for completing a quick task, and these are often simple enough to finish in under two minutes.

Priority: What to Buy First

Don't blow your early CR on flashy supercars. Instead, buy a reliable all-rounder like the Subaru WRX STI or Mazda MX-5 Miata (both excellent starter cars, see below). Put initial earnings into upgrades for ONE car rather than spreading money thin across many. Once that car is competitive in A-Class, expand to a drift build and a drag build.

Best Starter Cars: 5 Picks for New Players

The car you choose shapes your early FH6 experience. These five recommendations cover different playstyles — road racing, drifting, touge, and all-around usability — and all are affordable within your first few hours of play.

Car Year Class Drive Price Why It Rocks
Mazda MX-5 Miata (2016) 2016 D RWD ~35,000 CR Legendary handling balance. Lightweight, intuitive, and teaches proper weight transfer. A joy on touge roads.
Subaru WRX STI (2018) 2018 C AWD ~75,000 CR Fantastic AWD grip. Excellent in foul weather and mountain roads. Committed to touge culture for good reason.
Nissan 180SX (1997) 1997 D RWD ~50,000 CR The iconic drift chassis. Cheap, tuneable, and immediately satisfying when you master clutch kick initiation.
Toyota GR Supra (2020) 2020 A RWD ~850,000 CR A longer-term goal. Fast out of the box, grows with upgrades, and looks incredible in Photo Mode.
Ford Mustang GT (2018) 2018 B RWD ~350,000 CR American muscle, Japanese roads. High power, planted feel, and surprisingly good touge if tuned for grip.

How to Choose Your First Car

Ask yourself: what driving style excites me most? If you want to learn drift technique, start with the Nissan 180SX or 180SX Type X — they're rear-wheel drive, affordable, and respond to every input. If you prefer grip driving and championships, the Subaru WRX STI delivers confidence from day one. If you want to cruise and enjoy scenery, the MX-5 Miata is unmatched for feel and accessibility.

Frequently Asked Questions

Should I upgrade my starter car or buy a new one first?
Upgrade one car fully before buying another. FH6's upgrade system is deep — spreading money across multiple cars means none of them reach competitive performance. A fully upgraded C or B-Class car outperforms a stock S-Class car. Start with the WRX STI or 180SX and build it up.
What's the fastest way to earn Credits early?
Complete Showcase events and Championship series for the highest CR-per-minute. PR Stunts also pay well and teach precision driving. Avoid spending hours doing free-roam exploration for CR — structured events are 3-4x more efficient.
Should I use automatic or manual transmission?
Start with automatic. When you're comfortable with the car's handling, switch to manual. Manual gives you clutch control for better launches and engine braking on touge descents — meaningful advantages in competitive racing. But learn the car first, then learn the transmission.
What are the best beginner tracks in FH6 Japan?
Start with the Hakone Circuit (city area) — wide and forgiving with long straights for learning top speed. Then move to Lake Shoji Circuit for a tighter technical feel. Save Mount Akagi touge passes for when you have at least 10 hours of seat time.
How do I unlock more car brands?
Some brands are locked behind Festival reputation tiers. As you level up your Festival by completing events, new brand showrooms unlock automatically. High-end brands like Ferrari, Porsche, and Lamborghini typically unlock around Festival Level 15-20.
What happens when I get stuck in the game?
For physical getting-stuck (collision), pull the handbrake and reverse — or restart the race from the pause menu. For progression issues, check that you're connected online to sync your Festival progress. Restarting the game also fixes most physics glitches.
